I have a lot of experience growing accounts on social media platforms and I've been doing it for a while. As the years have gone on, I've noticed some pernicious patterns that are hard to ignore, but it was only a few days ago where I stumbled upon the term *enshittification* that the whole thing clicked.

See, I've been running up accounts on TikTok, Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, LinkedIn and so on. If there's a social media platform out there, I've probably posted on it using accounts that my team is either contracted for or accounts that we own. As I've been doing this I've noticed the degradation of these platforms, which turns out, fits under the umbrella phenomena *enshittification*.

Here's the official definition:

> *Enshittification* is the pattern where online platforms, apps, and services decline in quality over time. Coined by author Cory Doctorow, it describes a three-stage process: first, platforms are good to users; second, they abuse users to favor business customers; and finally, they exploit business customers to maximize profits for themselves.

---

## A walk through it

Do you remember how fun Facebook was when it first came out? You could post to your "wall" and all your friends would see it. You could add anyone, all your friends, and you could become very well known in your area if you so chose. You could play the farming game or the mafia game or whatever other game you wanted. You might get the odd poke now and again, and maybe you didn't like the "1 like, 1 Prayer" posts, but at least you felt like you were hanging out in a cool online space.

Then what happened? They introduced the feed, where little by little they took your attention away from all the things you enjoyed and pushed it towards outrage, towards disgust. They kept showing you things and watching how you watched them. Before you knew it, you were trapped in an emotional casino and all your friends' posts disappeared into the void.

On top of that, you started to see advertisements. It started as just one ad for something simple like a workout plan, then an iron curl, then 10 iron curls, then 20 ads saying you're not good enough in some way. You couldn't quite put your finger on it, but you knew that this whole experience was much worse than before.

As I got older, I got into posting content myself, mostly on the behalf of other people. At first I could tell how many views a video would get by the amount of followers I had. Then at some stage it became decoupled and it was all about how "good" the video was. Then it became about how many people would comment on the video, then how many shared it. The pattern kept changing and everything became less and less predictable. One day you might wake up to 10,000 likes and the next day 10. Almost like a... casino.

While I was doing this, I also made some friends who were in e-commerce. You know — bros who run ads on Facebook to get middle-aged Americans to click on websites that ship them Chinese products. These were very skilled people, and even they would tell me about how expensive Facebook ads have become and how their accounts were getting banned with no explanation. I didn't pay much attention to it, but just how I, as a user and content creator, was getting squeezed and abused, so were my e-commerce friends.

---

## How to enshittify

1. Make the app good for people
2. Make the app worse for people and better for businesses
3. Make the app worse for people *and* businesses to extract maximum value from all parties
4. Die(?)

---

## So why the blog?

I've just come to realise that no matter what platform I'm posting on, it's going to be subject to enshittification. Huge companies can't help themselves. They give you a worse and worse experience in order to give themselves more and more profit.

I thought about the platforms I was on and how I would spend months growing an account to a sizeable amount of followers, only for the platforms to switch up on me and not show my content to the people that follow me.

I came to realise that this whole social media game has now changed. **You don't "build" on social media anymore, you extract.** Because that's what these companies are doing to you.

You take whatever audience you have and you migrate them to places you own — that could be your newsletter, self-hosted community, or even a blog.

The point of the social media account is to promote your self-hosted, self-owned platforms for communicating to the people that want to hear from you.
